Things to see with
Pocket Universe
Now you have Pocket Universe, what do you do with it? Easy – you use it to find your way around the sky. Unlikea map on a piece of paper, the Pocket Universe display is dynamic. It shows you what you really see, for any time or location.

Zoom out until you see the horizon and get your bearings. It can take few moments to match the of the stars in the sky to the display you see. After all, you're trying to reconcile the huge vastness of the universe with a small LCD screen a few inches across!

To get started, try finding a few basic constellations. Those which you can see will depend on your location and the time of year. For Northern Hemisphere dwellers, Orion, Ursa Major (the "Big Dipper" or "Plough"), and Cassiopeia are good examples. They are obvious outlines which should help you gauge the size of objects in the sky. Remember that the two "pointer star" in Ursa Major point towards Polaris: a star which is always to the North of you. This will help you work out which way you are facing.

Once things have clicked into place and you have got to grips with the sky and using Pocket Universe to look around it, here is a list of some objects you might want to look out for. All these objects look best through a telescope (even a small one will do) or a pair of binoculars, although you can usually see something of interest with the naked eye on a good, clear, dark night.

Warning: don't look at the Sun through a telescope or binoculars. The heat and intensity of the Sun will permanently damage your eyes.

The Moon

The moon is our companion in space. As the Earth orbits the Sun, the Moon orbits the Earth. By quirk of nature, both the Moon and Sun appear to be the same size in the sky: although the Moon is very much smaller than the Sun, it's also very much closer.

The best time to look at the Moon is not when it's Full. The surface of the Full Moon is very bright, but there are no shadows: at other phases, the sunlight on the lunar surface casts shadows making it easier to see the craters, mountain ranges, and "seas". There are no real seas on the Moon of course, but early astronomers imaging that the smooth, darker regions on the Moon were oceans, lakes and even marshes – hence names such as "The Sea of Tranquillity": the location where mankind first explored the lunar surface back in 1969.

The Planets

The planets are the worlds which orbit the Sun. The Earth is only one of nine, and it's possible to see almost all the others if you know where to look. Venus, Jupiter and Saturn are particularly bright: appearing brighter than any star at times. Looking at them through a telescope, you should see clearly that they aren't like stars – rather than appearing like a point of light, they look like a small disk or crescent.

Jupiter is worth looking at for many reasons. First of all, it's the largest planet and so even a small telescope will resolve its shape. A telescope of 6" or more should clearly make patterns on its surface visible: usually two dark horizontal bands. These are variations in the planet's atmosphere. You should also see four bright dots close by: these are the moons of Jupiter, which orbit around it. You can use Pocket Universe to predict the location of these moons.

Saturn is the second largest planet, and although you probably won't see any surface detail you will see its beautiful rings. You must make a point of buying, begging or borrowing a telescope to see these!

Venus is the planet closest to us, and often appears as a bright star in the evening or morning. As it's surface is shrouded with clouds you won't see any detail: but you will see prominent phases. Venus will vary from a full disk to a thin crescent.

Mars is the "Red Planet", and it will appear this way in even a small telescope. With higher magnifications you should be able to detect the ice at the polar caps, and even some dark smudges on the surface. Mars suffers from huge periodic sand storms, so it's worth observing it from week to week.

Stars

No matter how powerful your telescope, stars always appear as points of light. They are just so far away that it is impossible to see any detail. However, that doesn't make them uninteresting: on the contrary, there are lots of stars worth your attention. Here are some worth looking out for. Their locations are provided as approximate Right Ascension and Declination values, so you can use Pocket Universe to find them.

Albireo is a star in the constellation Cygnus the Swan. It's interesting because it's actually a double star, one blue-green, the other yellow. The stars' proximity makes the colour contrast very obvious. RA: 19:29, Dec: 27:52) Other double/multiple stars include Mizar (RA: 12:54, Dec: 56:01) and Betelgeuse ( RA: 5:55, Dec: 7:23).

As you would expect there are thousands of other "interesting" stars, including variables which change their brightness periodically. Some variable stars change because they are eclipsed by fainter stars, others actually change brightness due to internal processes. Some stars which change in this way are located at RA: 00:38, Dec: 56:16. RA: 00:54, Dec: 60:27, RA: 01:06, Dec:-55:31, and RA: 02:17, Dec:-03:12.

Galaxies and Nebulae

Galaxies are huge collections of stars,. Our own sun is in a Galaxy too, which we call "The Milky Way". All the stars you can see with your naked eye are part of the Milky Way. On dark nights you can see the Milky Way appear as a fuzzy band of light crossing the entire sky: we see the band of light was we are looking edge-on into our own galaxy.

Looking for examples is easy: you don't need a telescope to see the brightest. The Andromeda Galaxy (M31) is a galaxy of a similar shape to our own, however it is over 2,200,000 light years away from us. That means when you look at it, the light you see has been travelling through space for over two million years. With the naked eye, you should be able to see a faint fuzzy patch: through binoculars or a telescope you should be able to see a shape with slightly more structure to it. Use the Goto Messier menu option, and enter M31 to find it.

Other Galaxies worth looking for include: M81 and M82 in Ursa Major, M104 in Virgo known as the "Sombrero Hat", M51known as the "Whirlpool" and M64 known as the "Black Eye". Please note that Galaxies by their nature are a long way away, and the light from them is faint. You may have seen spectacular photographs on them in books, but these have been taking using time exposures which can capture a lot of light. Even though large telescopes, Galaxies tend to look like fuzzy shapes: so don't be disappointed. Just think: when you look at M64 for example, the light started on its journey to your eye over 20 million years ago.

A Nebula is a vast cloud of dust or gas in a galaxy. There are many different kinds: some emit their own light, some absorb it and some reflect it. They can appear as coloured clouds, or black bands or even beautiful rings surrounding stars. Perhaps the most famous Nebula is the Orion Nebula (M42), which looks like a fuzzy glow under the "belt" of three stars.

Clusters

A group of stars held together by their mutual gravitational attraction is a cluster. They can appear as fuzzy patches, although they are actually composed of individual stars. Perhaps the most famous is the Pleiades, or Seven Sisters. This is a wonderful grouping of bright stars in the constellation of Taurus, also known as M45. You should have no trouble finding it, and it makes a wonderful site through binoculars.

The M13 cluster in Hercules and M44 Praesepe (Beehive) cluster are also bright enough to see with the naked eye, and if you are lucky you should be able to see the M11 "Wild Duck" cluster too.
