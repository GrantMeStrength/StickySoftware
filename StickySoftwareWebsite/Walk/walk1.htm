How to use...
Pocket Universe
Thinking of buying Pocket Universe? Maybe you are already a user? Here's a quick guide to getting started with the Palmsize PC version. All the steps will work on the Handheld PC version as well, although of course the screen display will look a little different!

If you would like a specific example of using Pocket Universe, please email and we'll do our best to accommodate you. Remember there is free email support for registered owners. universe@sticky.net

 

Walkthrough

When you launch Pocket Universe for the first time,
you'll see this splash screen. It contains a different
useful tip each time it appears, but you can select
not to have it open again. You can display this
window at any time from the File/About menu option.

Before you can use Pocket Universe, you need
to define two important settings: your location, and
the time. This is easily achieved by accessing the
standard pull-down menus.

You must enter your location in terms of latitude
and longitude, and also enter a TimeZone value.
The view of the sky will vary dramatically depending
on where you are situated.

If that seems like hard work, pop down the example
cities box to at least get a rough idea. Later on you
can use an atlas or even the Windows CE World
Clock to find more accurate values.

Now you can set the time. When you load
Pocket Universe, it will read the time from
the computer's own clock. You can enter any
date here if you want to see what the sky looked
like days, months or years in the past or future.
If your country is operating a Daylight Saving
scheme (such as British Summer Time) click
the box.

That's the hard part done: and remember you
don't need to do this every time. Click on the
Goto button, and let's see where the Sun is
currently in the sky.

You can go to three types of objects: items in the
solar system (the Sun, Moon and planets), named
stars and named constellations. Click in the Solar
System box, and select Sun.

The Sun will be centred in the display. In this view
you can just see the horizon at the bottom of the
screen.

For a better view, try this: click on Start, then
Settings, then Taskbar. Click on Autohide.
Click on OK.This turns off the Windows CE
taskbar.

The view from Pocket Universe now fills the entire
screen. But what is that to the upper right of the
sun? Click on it to centre it in the display.

Press the Action key, or select Toolbox from the
View menu. This opens up the floating control panel.
We are going to use the Zoom buttons or slider,
highlighted above.

Use the Magnifying buttons to zoom in to the
strange object, or click and drag the vertical slider.
Suddenly it becomes clear: the object is actually
two planets, Mercury and Venus. These planets
are usually close to the Sun.

Zoom out and click on the Outline button. This
draws and names the Constellations in the sky,
ideal for learning their shapes and names.Next
use Goto and this time visit the Moon. You can see
that the Moon's phase is clearly about three quarters
full.

Let's try something clever. Click on the Set Update
option from the View menu. Copy these settings,
making sure you have locked onto the Moon.

Now open the Display Settings (from the button in the
Command Bar, or from the View menu), and copy
these values. This switches off the horizon and
so prevents the Moon from being hidden.

Click on the Clock button, and the time will
advance by one day automatically. Watch how
the Moon's phase changes over time. You can
manually advance the time from the toolbox,
or by defining keys.

Remember, for more advice read the
online Help.

For Palmsize PC:

Select the Help button from the Taskbar. Click on
Start to bring up the menu, then click on Help.
If you have selected Autohide, you can click and
hold your stylus in the very bottom of the screen
to make the Taskbar appear.

For Handheld PC:

Click on Help Topics from the Help menu.
Alternatively, press CTRL and H to open the
help window.
