What is a Palmsize PC?

A small, hand-held computer running the Windows CE operating
system from Microsoft. Palmsize PCs are similar to their larger
brothers, the handheld PCs, in that they use the same operating
system (which means their screen displays look similar) and
come with similar software.

They don't have a keyboard, and instead use the touch screen
to pick out letters or understand your hand-writing. They come
with all the software and cables you need to link them up to your
computer, and at least 4Mb of memory (which is quite a lot) is
included as standard.

They all have back-lit, touch-sensitive, grey-scale screens
with a resolution of 240 by 320 pixels, built-in speaker and
microphone and an Infrared Port (IrDA compatible).

Why would I want one?

If you are a gadget fan, you don't even need to ask that question.
However, normal people (and apparently there are a few normal
people reading this page) might want to know why they should
consider spending a not inconsiderable sum buying a small battery
powered box.

The main reason is that the Palmsize PC is a PIM: a Personal
Information Manager. It's an easy way to carry around with you
all your names and address, telephone numbers, important dates,
day-planning information. The kind of stuff you would keep in a
leather-bound notebook. Keeping it on a little computer means that
it's a lot easier to keep up to date, and a little easier to access.

Is that all?

No -- far from it. Here's a list of the things which a PalmsizePC can
do. For many people it's something (or everything) from this list
which makes it worth buying.

  • Easily shares information with a desktop PC
  • Records voice notes, like a dictaphone
  • Can be used to receive and send email
  • Plays games
  • Can be used to scribble notes and make lists
  • Understands your handwriting
  • Can respond to voice commands: ask it to read out a
    phone number, for example.
  • Displays "smart" maps of most US cities. (Not yet Europe, grrrr)
  • Run applications, utilities and games written by third party
    companies (such as Sticky Software).

Who makes them?


Various models of PsPCs are available from manufactures
such as Philips, Casio and Everex. Here at Sticky Software
we use the Philip's Nino and Casio E-10, both of which we
think are excellent.

Aren't they a bit like Palm Pilots?

Yes! We happen to think they are better than Palm Pilots
for the following reasons:

  • Easy to customise (add your own graphics, sounds etc)
  • More powerful operating system
  • Comes with Web access (or a kind) and Email access as standard
  • Faster processor
  • Larger screen
  • More flexible input methods
  • Standard IrDA port
  • More expandable (Compact Flash support)
  • Voice recording as standard
  • Very well integrated into Windows
