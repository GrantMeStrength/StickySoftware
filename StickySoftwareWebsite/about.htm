View from
The Jar

As you might have guessed, Sticky Software isn't like other software companies. You might suspect that the people who run Sticky Software aren't like ordinary people. In fact, truth be told, they aren't like ordinary people. They aren't even people! There's just me: a disembodied brain floating in a jar of nutrients.

Live StickyCAM Picture

Despite what you might think, generally my day is very productive: as long as Mrs Sticky remembers to top up the jar with hydroponics solution and keep the cat out of my office, I can get a lot of work done. Thanks to the ECG/RJ11 card which came as an optional extra for this model of jar, I can communicate directly with this Windows PC, and so design web pages or fire up Visual C++ for a spot of programming.

This month for example, I had the joy of receiving a new piece of Windows CE hardware: a Vadem Clio. This is the computer with the large, flip screen which allows the unit to be used either as a desktop computer or a handheld tablet (or a funny easel mode for simulating a huge-screen TV to Barbie dolls). Side by side with the Jornada 820 I would have a very difficult job trying to decide which to buy. I like the fact that the Jornada has support for the REX, but the Clio is so much more interesting.

Speaking of Jornadas, the new HP colour palmsize PC is now on-sale in the US, and should reach these shores by the end of this month. Can't wait! Colour makes the displays so much easier to read, and makes games so much more interesting. I've plans for some great entertainment software – it's about time there was something other than tedious card games to play!

You might be wondering what kind of day-to-day work a brain in a tank could possible perform. I'll tell you: computer magazine journalism. It's easy – as a freelance, I never need to turn up to an office (and risk embarrassing spills, or worse, being mistaken for a water cooler). All I have to do is examine at new software and hardware (or read the press releases instead), write an article and email it in. A month later, a cheque arrives in the post. I've been doing this now for over ten years, and no-one has rumbled.

Sometimes editors ask for a photograph (generally new editors, who want to publish pictures of all their staff as an excuse to print their own photograph, twice the size, on the opening editorial page). I usually get a male model to stand-in for me, and this certainly increases the number of emails I get!

Brain,
