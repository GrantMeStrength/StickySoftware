REVIEW
Cassiopeia E-10



Please note: In the UK at least, the E-10 has been
replaced by the E-11. This is a functionally indentical
Palmsize PC, but with 8Mb instead of 4Mb and a
PSU included in the box. The price remains the
same, which could push it ahead of the Nino. Make
sure you see both before buying!

Small but perfectly formed?

Casio was the first out with a Palmsize PC, although not in
Europe where the Nino beat in into the shops. In fact, you
still might have to shop around a little to find an E-10
on sale in the UK. If you can get to Tottenham Court Road
your laughing, otherwise ring around a few dealers and be
prepared for mail order. This is a great shame, as the E-10
is one heck of a little machine.

And it most definitely is little too: much smaller than the Nino,
and much closer to the Palm Pilot which used to grace this
very household. Part of the size is due to the use of AAA
rather than AA batteries. This makes a difference to
battery life of course, and there is a warning in the
instructions regarding the use of the backlight and sound
recorder. The first thing I did when the E-10 arrived was to
order a set of NiHM cells.

Tediously, no PSU is included as standard. If your PsPC
is going to spend a lot of time parked in its docking bay
and connected to your desktop PC, then a PSU is very
useful. It's hard to accept there isn't a PSU in the box:
I had to keep checking in case I'd missed it. But no, it's
an optional extra.

The E-10 has a slightly different set of controls from the
Nino. Rather than a set of buttons on the side, the action,
up and down keys are in th form of a "rocker" switch. It's
easy to use, and slightly more intuative that bare buttons.
Right-handed people can easily use their thumb to control
it, Left-handies an index finger. Just above the rocker
switch is the voice record activation button. Of course, it's
just another user-definably button, but separating it from the
other three buttons on the front panel makes it a little more
special.

Different, by design

Once you get over the slightly different controls and smaller
size, it's hard to tell the Nino and Cassiopeia apart. Except
that is when an intensive program such as Pocket Universe.
Although the E-10 also uses a MIPS processor, it's a
slightly different one from that in the Nino. To quote Casio,

"..the on chip cache size is 8x that on the Nino so for
computationally intensive aps this would gain advantage.
Also the system board has been designed to take much
faster processors so minimised wait states."

Sounds good, but it basically means it ran the astronomy
application just that little bit fastert. Very impressive.
The other Windows CE applications seemed to run at much
the same speed, if slightly nippier.. Another difference is the
inclusion of an earphone socket for listening to voice notes
without annoying your colleagues.

Bundle

Casio pop a CD of software for loading onto your E-10 into
the box, and the list includes bFax Express, a financial
calculator, a useful Find utility and a program for linking
with Casio digital cameras. Sadly the Tetris deal seems
to be over, as this isn't included.

The E-10 has 4Mb of memory as standard. If you want to
use the Casio for PIM duties, this is more than enough. If
you want to install lots of third party software, then budget
for a CF card: even a small 4Mb card makes all the
difference.

Conclusion

In the handheld PC market, size does matter: and the E-10
has that in its favour. It lack of PSU is annoying, and the
battery life is reduced -- although that's easily fixed with a
set of rechargables. Again, it's easy to fill that 4Mb, although
adding a CF card more than makes up for it. I'm actually
torn between the speed and size of the E-10, and the style
of the Nino. If Casio added rechargable batteries and a PSU,
then I'd probably pick it. It's cute!

Pros

  • Small size
  • Speed
  • Screen protector included
  • Software bundle

Cons

  • Lack of PSU
  • Lack of rechargable batteries
  • Annoying rubber bung over the docking port
  • Nasty tendency to switch itself off when batteries
    are less than 100% and backlight and/or sound event
    occurs.
