REVIEW
Philips Nino300

Form over function?

The Nino has style, there's no doubt about it. Philips have
a exciting design department, not afraid to make their
products look different, and the Philip's Palmsize PC
has benefited.

The hardware specs are standard for the PsPC, with
a MIPS processor, 4Mb of memory, 320 by 240 four
grey-scale screen, and a stylus. The state of the modem
is open to question: it's possible that the European
models will use a soft-modem via a simple clip-in lead,
but as yet this has to be confirmed. Personally, I use
the IR port to communicate with an Ericsson SH888
mobile phone, so I'm totally spoilt.

Speed is good, and all the standard WindowsCE
applications included work perfectly well. 4Mb mightn't
seem a lot, but it's still enough for all the contacts,
tasks and appointments you are likely to need. If you
do intend to use a lot of third party software (and I
certainly hope you will!) a Compact Flash card makes a
great investment. I've fitted a 32Mb card snugly and
discretely in the slot on my Nino, and still have to hit
half full: and that is with four full novels, a lot of sampled
voice notes, and all the software I can get my hands on
installed. Yup, the 4Mb basic model suits me fine.

Bundles of fun

The bundled software is a welcome surprise with the Nino.
Unlike other systems, it comes built-into ROM, which
means you can enjoy the advantages of smART's voice
and hand writing recognition packages from the moment
you switch on. The T9 soft keyboard is also included,
although I have to admit I'm not a fan of this system.

Another pleasant surprise was the inclusion of a NiMH
battery pack as standard. No need to worry about your
batteries running out, even with the backlight on all the
time. As long as the Nino is returned to its lovely little
docking platform, it will recharge overnight and be ready
for action in the morning.

The carrying case for the Nino says Samonsite on it,
although it's leather-ette material and not solid plastic
designed in a wind tunnel, or designed to withstand
being run over in a truck. It will prevent scratches and
does allow access to the voice recording button. It's
also slightly bulky due to ridged edges, and you might
find it too large to fit into a pocket. I've risked carrying
it without the case, but will acquire some screen
protectors before making that a regular occurance.
Speaking of the screen, I've no complaints about it,
and that is sitting it side-by-side with the Casio E-10.
Both screens are extremely similar.

Conclusion

The Nino is definitely a well thought-out machine. Philips
have ensure that out of the box, it comes with everything
you need. Three hundred pounds is a lot of money, but
you are getting a lot of computer for your money. More and
more people are realising that Windows CE is much more
than a PIM (personal information manager) and the Nino is
an ideal machine to jump into the world of mobile computing.

Pros

  • Looks wonderful
  • Comes with NiMH rechargable battery pack as standard
  • PSU included as standard
  • Useful software built-in

Cons

  • Slightly larger than expected
  • 4Mb is small if using other apps: buy a CF card

Accessories

  • Spare PC connection leads
  • CF cards
